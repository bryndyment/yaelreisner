Abstract

A fresh take on Beauty in architecture is evolving from the focus on human wellbeing as an important element of sustainable ecosystems. It’s augmented by new findings in neurobiology: When we look at things we consider to be beautiful, there is a great deal of dopamine in the pleasure reward centers of the brain, the ‘feel-good’ transmitter, explains Semi Zeki.  Apparently, Beauty is an essential part of our neurobiological make-up.


That goes well with the fact that good architecture tends to be judged by its capacity to produce an aesthetic experience, yet architectural design for years was often neither led by nor generated through a process engaged with aesthetical issues or led by visual thinking.


In parallel, from mass production, to digital fabrication and mass customization, the question of expression has remained heavily generated by the functional as its main driver.

Advanced computational methods and generative strategies become seamlessly coupled with digital fabrication, where robotic paths leading towards a much higher efficiency in material spent, where material´s mass is the main design constrain. Hence the modernist mythos ‘repetition=economic’ becomes obsolete.


These technological advancements not only enable us investigating the use of a wider range of materials and giving us much higher degree of freedom in exploring new aesthetics, but they also allow the emergence of new hybrid materials where organic (seeds for example) and inorganic can become harmoniously integrated at all stages of the design process. Thus plants could become even more integral part of architecture.

We gear up for designing ecological systems while believing that sustainability fails without Beauty.
